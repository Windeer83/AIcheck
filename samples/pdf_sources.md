## 01_sleep_memory_study.pdf

Title: Sleep Duration and Memory Retention in Adult Learners

Authors: Chen, Li and Wang

Year: 2024

Abstract:

This controlled study examined the relationship between sleep duration and next-day vocabulary recall in adult learners. Eighty adult learners were randomly assigned to a 5-hour sleep opportunity or an 8-hour sleep opportunity after studying a list of 120 foreign-language vocabulary pairs.

Methods:

Participants completed a delayed recall test the next morning. The 8-hour sleep group had a mean vocabulary recall rate of 86 percent. The 5-hour sleep group had a mean vocabulary recall rate of 71 percent. The difference between groups was 15 percentage points. Mean reaction time during the recall task was 420 milliseconds in the 8-hour sleep group and 460 milliseconds in the 5-hour sleep group.

Conclusion:

Longer sleep duration was associated with better next-day vocabulary retention in this sample. The study did not test caffeine intake, mindfulness training, cortisol, or long-term academic grades.

## 02_caffeine_sleep_latency_trial.pdf

Title: Evening Caffeine Intake and Sleep Latency: A Randomized Crossover Trial

Authors: Patel and Gomez

Year: 2023

Abstract:

This randomized crossover trial evaluated the effect of evening caffeine intake on sleep onset latency in healthy adults. Thirty-six participants received 200 mg caffeine or placebo three hours before bedtime on separate study nights.

Methods:

Compared with placebo, 200 mg caffeine increased mean sleep onset latency from 14 minutes to 31 minutes. Total sleep time was reduced by 42 minutes after caffeine. The trial did not measure vocabulary recall, next-day memory performance, or academic achievement.

Conclusion:

Evening caffeine delayed sleep onset and reduced total sleep time. The findings do not support caffeine as an intervention for faster sleep onset.

## 03_mindfulness_stress_pilot.pdf

Title: Six-Week Mindfulness Training and Perceived Stress: A Pilot Study

Authors: Huang et al.

Year: 2022

Abstract:

This pilot study tested a six-week mindfulness training program in university students. Forty-two participants completed weekly group sessions and daily home practice.

Methods:

The mean PSS-10 perceived stress score decreased from 22.0 at baseline to 18.0 after six weeks, representing an 18 percent reduction. The study did not include 500 participants. The study did not measure salivary cortisol, sleep efficiency, vocabulary recall, or academic grades.

Conclusion:

Mindfulness training was associated with a modest reduction in perceived stress in a small pilot sample. Larger trials are needed before making broad claims about academic performance.
